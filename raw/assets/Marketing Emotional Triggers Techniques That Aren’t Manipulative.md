### Psychological Trigger #1: Social Proof

The first powerful psychological trigger I want to talk about is Social Proof. You see this one everywhere: social media comments, online reviews, testimonials and case studies.

![social proof examples](https://i0.wp.com/copyposse.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/social-proof-examples.png?w=1634&ssl=1)

social proof examples

Social proof is a psychological and social phenomenon in which people look to others to guide behaviour and decision-making, especially in uncertain situations, like when making a purchase.

It operates on the assumption that “other people” possess more knowledge about a situation, causing you to conform to group behavior.  
  
It works because our brains are wired for survival. For thousands of years, following the crowd literally kept us alive. If everyone else was running from the sabre-tooth tiger, you’d better run too.

Now obviously there is a douchey and an ethical way to use this trigger.

The douchey way involves using fake testimonials, fake followers, fake reviews, fake success stories.  
  
And, in my opinion, this also includes fake or AI-generated comments and user engagement. Faking social proof in any way is highly unethical.

**The ethical way involves sharing genuine feedback, experiences and comments from real clients and customers.**

![social proof testimonials](https://i0.wp.com/copyposse.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/social-proof-testimonials-scaled.png?resize=2048%2C1113&ssl=1)

social proof testimonials

But here’s the key. You don’t just want to share the amazing “you changed my life, 10/10 recommend!” type of social proof. Don’t get me wrong, that’s great, but don’t forget to share the “before” along with the “after”.

When you share your customers’ entire journey – from where they started to the specific struggles and doubts they faced along the way – the social proof becomes way more persuasive, believable and relatable.

### Psychological Trigger #2: Scarcity

Also known as FOMO or the fear of missing out.

Scarcity is the fundamental economic principle of supply and demand, or more specifically, the gap between demand and supply, and how that impacts human behavior.

When something is scarce or limited, we want it way more than if it’s in abundant supply. The more available something is, the less valuable it is.

In marketing, scarcity is the principle of making products or services seem limited in some way, and therefore harder to obtain. This works because FOMO is real. We don’t want to miss out on an opportunity to have something or experience something that’s scarce or exclusive.

By making the offer limited in some way, it incentivizes your customer to act now.

#### There are four different types of scarcity you could use in your sales messaging.

**Type #1: Price Scarcity**

This the good ol’ fashion limited-time discount. We are very familiar with this one.

You see this type of scarcity used all the time during holidays, right? Labor Day sales, Black Friday sales, and on and on…

![price scarcity examples with 50% graphic](https://i0.wp.com/copyposse.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/price-scarcity.png?w=800&ssl=1)

price scarcity examples with 50% graphic

This is where you offer a discounted price for a limited time.  
  
Or you might use…

#### Type #2: Quantity Scarcity

Which is exactly what it sounds like: a limited quantity of what you’re offering.

This type of scarcity works very well with physical products – where there actually is a limited number of units in inventory.

![quantity scarcity example email](https://i0.wp.com/copyposse.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/quantity-scarcity.png?w=1312&ssl=1)

quantity scarcity example email

This could also be used with exclusive experiences like the number of spots in a coaching program or a retreat or an event, something where the attendance is capped at a specific number.

The next type of scarcity is…

#### Type #3: Premium Scarcity

This is when you add in limited-time bonuses. So you have to buy by a certain time and date in order to get them.

Here you are temporarily upgrading the value of your offer and adding scarcity at the same time.

![premium scarcity email example](https://i0.wp.com/copyposse.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/premium-scarcity.png?w=1078&ssl=1)

premium scarcity email example

And the last type of scarcity you might choose to use is…

#### Type #4: Offer Scarcity

Unlike price scarcity, where your prospects are expecting an impending rise in price, or premium scarcity where bonuses disappear, or quantity scarcity where you only have a certain number of products or spots available.

Offer scarcity is when the offer itself is closing and will then be completely unavailable for an extended period of time.

I use this type of scarcity with my [live Launch Pad Coaching Program](http://www.copyposse.com/launchpad) every single year, right? I only open enrollment once a year, so when the doors close, that’s it. You missed it and you have to wait an entire year for doors to open again.

![offer scarcity example](https://i0.wp.com/copyposse.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/offer-scarcity.png?w=1258&ssl=1)

offer scarcity example

Now just like the first trigger, there is a douchey way of using scarcity and an ethical way.

The douchey way is when your scarcity is fake. Fake deadlines, fake countdowns, fake inventory numbers or high-pressure tactics that make people panic-buy.

**If you want to use scarcity the ethical way – when you say something is limited – mean it. Be truthful and honest**.

Pretty simple, right?!

Alright, moving on to the third trigger…

### Psychological Trigger #3: Authority

Creating authority is the strategic process of positioning a brand or individual as a leading, trusted expert within a specific industry. Authority is a major psychological trigger because we’re programmed to follow leaders and trust experts. It’s why we listen to doctors about our health and mechanics about our cars.

To build authority in your messaging, you might talk about how many people you’ve helped, your experience, degrees, certifications or media features.

Now, obviously, doing things like claiming expertise you don’t actually have, listing fake credentials, or renting a Lambo just so you can take pictures in it and pretend it’s yours is a douchey way to use this trigger.

**To use this trigger ethically, you must share only your real expertise and experience.**

And if you don’t have any yet, that’s okay too! Sharing your story and mission is a great way to build trust and credibility, even if you are just getting started and don’t have “authority” metrics or features to showcase just yet. Everyone has to start somewhere.

Alright, and now for what just might be the most powerful trigger on the list…

### Psychological Trigger #4: Reciprocity

Ahhh yes. The universal urge for human beings to repay or reciprocate after an act of kindness or generosity by another. When someone does something nice for us, we feel obligated to return the favor. It’s basic human psychology. If you scratch my back, I’ll scratch yours. Simple as that.

You see this rooted in numerous cultures, traditions and rituals around the world, at weddings, between neighbors, in business relationships, and it’s also no stranger in the world of marketing.

The trigger or reciprocity is used when you incentivize prospects to take a desired action by giving them something of value first.

Think about it: this concept is baked into every single opt-in page used for lead generation.

I’ll give you something really cool and free – all ya gotta do is enter your email address and you’ll be showered with [free value and amazing content](https://copyposse.com/ai).

![lead magnets sign up pop up form](https://i0.wp.com/copyposse.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/lead-magnets-scaled.png?w=2000&ssl=1)

lead magnets sign up pop up form

It could be a complimentary 30-minute assessment call if you’re a coach. Or a free trial for your software or subscription service — such as Spotify and YouTube Premium’s 30-day trials.

You even see non-profit organizations do this by giving donors free merchandise in return for their donation, increasing the likelihood that they will donate again.

The douchey way of using this trigger would be to use “bait and switch” tactics – in other words, promising value and not delivering it until they take yet another desired action.

**The ethical way is to give genuinely valuable content with no strings attached, because you actually want to help people, regardless of their next move.**  
  
Just like my [blog.](http://www.copyposse.com/blog) I spend hours creating content for you guys every single week, without any expectation of compensation. I truly truly enjoy this!

When you help people win unselfishly, they remember. So that when they’re ready to invest in themselves and take the next step, they’ll think of you first.
